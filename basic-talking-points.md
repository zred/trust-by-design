# Security Awareness Primer (1 Hour)

# Session Objectives (5 minutes)

By the end of this session, employees should:

- Understand how social engineering targets people
- Recognize emotional manipulation (RICE)
- Identify high-risk scenarios in our environment
- Know what to do when something feels suspicious
- Understand hardware supply chain risks in consumer and workplace settings
- Understand our safe harbor obligations regarding client DMS data

Reinforce:
Security awareness protects:
- Our clients
- Their customers
- Our contracts
- Our reputation
- Each other

---

# 1. Our Risk Landscape (10 minutes)

## Who We Are

We are a second-party partner with access to:
- Client DMS data
- Consumer lead data
- Campaign analytics
- Platform credentials
- Vendor billing and ad spend systems

## Why That Matters

Client DMS data may include:
- Vehicle ownership information
- Service records
- Customer contact information
- Transaction histories

Under safe harbor expectations:
- We are trusted custodians
- Improper exposure impacts our clients, not just us
- Contractual and regulatory consequences may follow

Key Point:
Attackers do not need to breach a dealer directly if they can breach a vendor.

# 2. How Social Engineering Works (10 minutes)

## Social Engineering Defined

Manipulating people to bypass security controls.

It targets:
- Trust
- Urgency
- Authority
- Routine behavior

Most breaches begin with:
- A click
- A credential
- A payment change
- An access request

## Common Techniques

- Phishing (email, SMS, messaging apps)
- Pretexting (fabricated scenarios)
- Impersonation (executives, vendors, IT)
- Urgency/pressure tactics
- Conversation hijacking

Familiar does not equal safe.

---

# 3. RICE: Recognizing Emotional Manipulation (10 minutes)

RICE stands for:
- Reward
- Intimidation
- Convenience
- Ego

Attackers trigger emotion to reduce critical thinking.

## Reward
- Bonuses, refunds, exclusive access

## Intimidation
- Account suspension, legal threats, compliance pressure

## Convenience
- "This will only take a minute"
- Bypassing ticketing or approval processes

## Ego
- Flattery
- "Only you can handle this"

## Pause Signal
If you feel urgency, fear, excitement, or pressure — stop and verify.

# 4. Tactics You May Not Expect (15 minutes)

## Littering
- USB drives in parking lots or break rooms
- Printed "confidential" documents
- QR codes in shared spaces

## Cloning
- Lookalike invoices
- Duplicate login pages
- Slightly altered domains

## Conversation Hijacking
- Payment changes mid-thread
- "Updated" attachments
- Subtle instruction shifts

## Environmental Trust Exploitation
- Branded clothing or badges
- Industry-specific language
- Unchallenged physical access

## Hardware Supply Chain

### Consumer Marketplaces
- Third-party sellers on Amazon, Walmart, and Best Buy Marketplace
- Commingled inventory — a bad actor's units can ship from a trusted listing
- High-risk categories: cables, adapters, hubs, keyboards, streaming devices

### Hardware Implants
- O.MG Cable — standard-looking USB cable containing a wireless implant (~$150–$200)
- USB Rubber Ducky — looks like a thumb drive, injects keystrokes (~$60)
- LAN Turtle — looks like a network adapter, provides persistent remote access (~$60)
- Bash Bunny — looks like a thumb drive, presents as multiple device types (~$120)

### BADBOX: Firmware-Level Compromise
- IoT devices shipped with malware pre-installed in firmware
- Affected categories: streaming boxes, projectors, digital picture frames
- FBI warning (June 2025): millions of devices affected across mainstream retail
- Survives factory resets; no visible symptoms

FBI warning signs:
- Requires disabling Google Play Protect during setup
- Sources apps from unofficial marketplaces
- Advertised as "unlocked" or offering free streaming content
- Brand is unrecognizable or unverifiable
- Unexplained outbound network traffic

Key Point:
Hardware supply chain threats do not require a user to make a mistake.
Procurement discipline and network controls are the defense.

# 5. High-Risk Scenarios in Our Environment (10 minutes)

## Vendor Invoice / Payment Changes
- Updated wire instructions
- New ACH details
- "Send to this new address"

Always verify through a second channel.

## Platform Access & MFA Reset Requests
- "I'm locked out"
- "I need quick access"
- "Can you share credentials?"

Never share passwords. Escalate reset requests through formal process.

## DMS Data Handling
- Unexpected export requests
- Large data pulls
- Requests for "raw lists"

Verify: Is this necessary? Is it approved? Is it consistent with safe harbor?

## Executive Urgency Requests
- Gift cards, wire transfers, confidential document requests

Executives expect process compliance.

---

# 6. Data Classification & Safe Harbor (5 minutes)

High sensitivity in our environment:
- Client DMS data
- Customer contact information
- Payment data
- Vendor contracts
- Access credentials

Do not:
- Upload client data to third-party analysis sites
- Share large exports without approval
- Email raw DMS data without authorization

If unsure: escalate before sharing.

---

# 7. What To Do (5 minutes)

When something feels suspicious:

1. Pause
2. Verify (use known contact info, not reply-in-thread)
3. Escalate to security or leadership
4. Preserve evidence

If you clicked or responded:
Report immediately. Speed matters more than embarrassment.

If you find unknown hardware or a suspicious device on a network drop:
- Do not plug it in
- Do not remove it if already connected
- Report to IT immediately using a separate device

# 8. Cultural Expectations (5 minutes)

You are allowed to:
- Say no
- Ask questions
- Slow down
- Escalate

You will not be penalized for verifying.

Security is not about suspicion. It is about protecting trust.
