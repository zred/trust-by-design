# Security Awareness Primer – Speaker Notes (1 Hour)

# Opening (5 Minutes)

## Objective

Today’s session is about protecting:

- Our clients
- Their customers
- Our contracts
- Our reputation
- Each other

Most breaches do not begin with advanced hacking.
They begin with a human decision made under pressure.

Security awareness is not paranoia.
It is operational discipline.

---

# Section 1 – Our Risk Landscape (10 Minutes)

## Our Role

We are a second-party partner with access to:

- Client DMS data
- Customer contact information
- Campaign systems
- Vendor billing systems
- Platform credentials

Attackers target vendors because:
- Vendors may have broader access across multiple clients.
- Vendors are often perceived as having fewer controls.

## Safe Harbor Considerations

Client DMS data may include:
- Vehicle ownership information
- Service records
- Customer contact details
- Transaction histories

We are custodians of this data.
Exposure impacts our clients directly.

Key message:
Compromising a vendor is often easier than compromising a dealership.

---

# Section 2 – How Social Engineering Works (10 Minutes)

Social engineering manipulates people to bypass controls.

It exploits:
- Urgency
- Authority
- Familiarity
- Desire to help
- Fear of delay

Common techniques:
- Phishing (email, SMS, messaging platforms)
- Impersonation (executives, vendors, IT)
- Pretexting (fabricated scenarios)
- Conversation hijacking

Reminder:
Familiar does not equal safe.

---

# Section 3 – Additional Tactics (10 Minutes)

## Littering
- USB drives in parking lots
- Printed “confidential” documents
- QR codes in shared areas

## Cloning
- Lookalike invoices
- Slightly altered domains
- Duplicate login pages

## Conversation Hijacking
- Payment changes mid-thread
- “Updated” attachments
- Subtle instruction shifts

## Environmental Trust Exploitation
- Branded clothing
- Industry language
- Unchallenged physical access

Key point:
The more normal something feels, the more important verification becomes.

---

# Section 4 – RICE Framework (10 Minutes)

RICE describes emotional triggers attackers use:

- Reward
- Intimidation
- Convenience
- Ego

## Reward
“You qualify for…”
“Exclusive access…”

## Intimidation
“Immediate action required…”
“Account suspension…”

## Convenience
“This will only take a minute…”
“No need to log a ticket…”

## Ego
“You’re the only one who can do this…”
“Keep this confidential…”

Pause Signal:
If you feel urgency, fear, excitement, or pressure — stop and verify.

---

# Section 5 – High-Risk Scenarios in Our Environment (10 Minutes)

## Vendor Payment Changes
Always verify using a second communication channel.

## Platform Access / MFA Reset
Never share credentials.
Escalate reset requests through formal process.

## DMS Data Handling
Large exports require:
- Business justification
- Approval
- Safe handling

## Executive Urgency Requests
Executives expect compliance with process.

---

# Section 6 – Data Handling & Safe Harbor (5 Minutes)

High sensitivity data includes:

- Client DMS exports
- Customer contact lists
- Payment data
- Vendor contracts
- Credentials

Do not:
- Upload client data to third-party analysis tools
- Share raw exports without approval

If unsure:
Escalate before acting.

---

# Section 7 – Operational Response Model (5 Minutes)

When something feels suspicious:

1. Pause
2. Verify (use known contact info)
3. Escalate
4. Preserve evidence

If you clicked:
Report immediately.
Speed matters more than embarrassment.

---

# Closing (5 Minutes)

Three habits:

Pause  
Verify  
Escalate  

Security awareness is operational maturity.
Trust is built through discipline.
